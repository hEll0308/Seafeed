Frequently Asked Questions

How do cattle produce methane?

A cow has 4 stomachs, and methane production takes place in the first stomach, which is known as the rumen. In the rumen, there are microbes called methanogens and they bind with vitamin B12 to digest food. However, as a byproduct, they produce a ton of methane which is belched out through the mouth. This process is called enteric fermentation.

How does the seaweed reduce methane emissions in from cattle?

Asparagopsis contains a chemical called bromoform, and what bromoform does is bind to the vitamin B12 itself, so that the methanogens cannot bind to it. Without vitamin B12, these microbes cannot undergo enteric fermentation, and therefore cannot produce methane.

What is currently being done to reduce cattle emissions?

There are companies using garlic, citric acid, herbs and even yeast to reduce methane emissions in cattle and ruminants. However, their products aren't nearly as efficient as Asparagopsis Taxiformis, only reducing emissions by up to 30%.

Are there any companies using Asparagopsis Taxiformis?

Yes there are other companies like Future Feed, Symbrosia and Greener Grazing. However, most of these companies are facing the issue of scaling seaweed production and none have yet to reach the market.

How much methane is being released by livestock and cattle each year?

r In total, livestock emits 284 million tonnes of methane each year, which accounts for 14.5% of all anthropogenic greenhouse gases. To compare, that's almost 94 million tanker trucks' worth of gasoline! Cattle approximately release 80-140 million tonnes of methane each year, which accounts for 8-10% of all anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions.

How much methane can Asparagopsis reduce in different doses?

With a >2% inclusion in a cattle's diet, Asparagopsis Taxiformis can reduce methane emissions in enteric fermentation by over 99% and with a 1% inclusion, can reduce around 95% of emissions. Some studies are even suggesting that 0.2% inclusion can reduce methane emissions by around 90%.

Does feeding seaweed negatively effect cattle?

Feeding Asparagopsis Taxiformis doesn't harm cattle in any physical way. In fact, the inclusion of Asparagopsis did not influence the tenderness, juiciness, flavor, or overall liking of the meat according to Meat Standards Australia (MSA) inspectors. No traces of seaweed or bromoform could be found in the meat, milk, kidney, fat, or feces either.

How can the seaweed increase the productivity of cattle?

10-15% of the cow's daily energy is used to produce methane when undergoing enteric fermentation. By eliminating the process of methane creation, the remaining 10-15% of energy is used for other things like producing more meat, which increases the productivity of the cattle.

What's the difference between Asparagopsis Taxiformis and Armata?

Currently, Seafeed is using Asparagopsis Taxiformis to reduce methane emissions in cattle and ruminant livestock. There are two main types of Asparagopsis seaweed which are Asparagopsis Taxiformis and Asparagopsis Armata. Taxiformis thrives in tropical waters while Armata thrives in temperate waters.

What's the difference between a ruminant and cattle?

Ruminants are animals that contain a rumen, which is a type of stomach. This includes cows, sheep, goats, and buffalo. Cattle are a type of ruminant that belongs to the genus, Bos. This includes cows and bulls exclusively.
